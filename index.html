Why Create?

I’ve always been drawn to creating. When I was younger, that meant sketching and experimenting with art, eventually teaching at an art academy where I learned to think more intentionally about design, detail, and how people interact with what they create.

Over time, that curiosity shifted from how things look to how they work. I became more interested in breaking systems down, understanding how they function, and building them back in a more thoughtful way. That mindset led me to Electrical Engineering at Georgia Tech, where I now focus on biotechnology and sensing, working on systems that capture and interpret real-world signals.

I’m especially interested in developing technologies that operate outside controlled environments—whether that’s in medical applications or exploration contexts like space or environmental sensing. I’m drawn to problems where systems need to interact directly with the real world, and where engineering can make those interactions more meaningful, reliable, and useful.

At the core, I create because I want to understand the world more deeply and have a hand in shaping how we interact with it. Creating is how I test ideas, challenge assumptions, and turn curiosity into something tangible. It’s not just about building something that works—it’s about building something that makes sense, something that feels intentional, and something that can exist meaningfully in the real world.
